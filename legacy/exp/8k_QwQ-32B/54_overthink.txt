1. **Faraday's Law and Time Derivative of B Inside the Superconductor**:  
   Inside the superconductor, the electric field \( \mathbf{E} \) is zero. Faraday's Law states that \( \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \). Since \( \mathbf{E} = 0 \) inside, the curl of \( \mathbf{E} \) must also be zero, which implies that \( \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} = 0 \) inside the superconductor. Thus, **the magnetic field \( \mathbf{B} \) inside is time-independent**.

2. **Continuity of \( \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \) Across the Boundary**:  
   The magnetic field \( \mathbf{B} \) is continuous in the tangential direction and can have a discontinuity in the normal direction across the superconductor surface (due to surface currents). However, the *time derivative* of \( \mathbf{B} \), \( \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \), must also be continuous across the boundary. Since \( \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} = 0 \) inside the superconductor, it must also be zero just outside the surface. This ensures the consistency of Faraday’s Law, which links \( \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \) to the curl of \( \mathbf{E} \).

3. **Implications for the Magnetic Field Outside**:  
   A non-zero time dependence of \( \mathbf{B} \) outside would violate the continuity of \( \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \), as the interior requires \( \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} = 0 \). Therefore, **\( \mathbf{B} \) must also be time-independent just outside the superconductor**.
